Building Robots
For People

Hi, I'm Madison! I am a second-year student at Stanford University pursuing degrees in Mechanical Engineering and Computer Science. I am passionate about problem solving and building the unknown. I am specifically interested in the intersection between humans, robotics, and Artificial Intelligence.

Beyond classes, I'm pursuing my interest primarily through research and personal projects. Having been interested in robotics and assistive devices, I am the only permanent undergraduate researcher in the Biomechatronics Lab. As a researcher I design and build prosthetics and exoskeletons, and create software for data analysis. Past projects include designing and printing 3D printed prosthetic hands and a 3D model of a skeletal hand.

I am also the Academic Development Co-Chair in Stanford Undergraduates in Mechanical Engineering (SUME). As a part of SUME, I work with the team to organize school-wide events to make SUME a "one-stop shop" for all things MechE.

I have strong technical skills with Fusion360 (CAD), additive manufacturing, and the application of programming languages, specifically MATLAB, C++, Python, and Java.

Research

Biomechatronics Lab under Professor Steve Collins

Split Belt Study [Publication in progress]

Ran a split-belt treadmill study with n=10 bilateral transtibial amputation (below-the-knee) as well as a n=10 matched control study to determine decreased metabolic cost.

Processed 20x3 hours worth of per-millisecond Ground Reaction Force (GRF) data from the treadmill to process step times, stance times, step lengths, metabolics, and comfort. Created a function that determines strides based on GRF data, heelstrikes, toeoff, and cleans up crossovers.

Presented at the SURI poster session

Wheelie [Publication in progress]

Ran a novel wheeled prosthesis study with n=10 bilateral transtibial amputation to determine metabolic cost.

Processed 10x4 hours worth of per-millisecond Ground Reaction Force (GRF) data from the treadmill to process step times, stance times, step lengths, metabolics, and comfort.

Stroke patient exoskeleton [Publication in progress]

Processed 10x2 hours worth of per-millisecond Ground Reaction Force (GRF) data from the treadmill to process mechanical power of the exoskeleton's ankle, knee flexion, and knee extension.

Manak Lab under Professor John Manak @ University of Iowa

Motor and Learning Deficits of Neurodegeneration, Specifically Epilepsy

Investigated prickle gene mutations in Drosophila larvae, assessing locomotion and olfactory learning to determine if deficits seen in adults also appear earlier in development. Findings showed that sple/sple larvae exhibited no deficits, while pk/pk larvae displayed unexpectedly faster crawling, suggesting stage-specific neurological effects of prickle mutations

Presented at SSTP Poster session
